    • Automated science?

    The list of human activities performed by machines has greatly expanded with recent developments in artificial intelligence, machine leaning, data mining and big data. On that list may now figure science and engineering.
    In 2009 a program developped at Cornell "discovered" Newton's second law (F = ma), without human input. This was accomplished by randomly generating algebraic expressions to model the motion of an oscillating pendulum, in combination with a genetic algorithm to identify the most insightful of these analytic formulations.

    Computer Program self-discovers laws of physics

    The program, now called Eurequa is one of the new generation of analytics plateforms (like IBM's Watson) that can be used for tasks traditionally performed by technicians, scientists, engineers and data scientists.
    With the ever more complex systems analyzed, and ever larger amounts of data available, these machines are seen as a great augmentation, if not replacement, to scientists and engineers that will increase efficiency and productivity in industries from drug development to aerospace design.

    In Douglas Adam's "Hitchhiker's guide to the Galaxy",Deep Thought, the advanced computer, after 7.5 millions years, was able to compute the answer to the Ultimate Question of Life, the Universe, and Everything. The answer was, incomprehensibly, 42.
    The automated computer scientist poses the same dilemna. Unless the machine also build theories and conceptual framework to understand the relations it discovered, it will be difficult to make sense, trust, and generalize the findings. With ver increasing complex problems being solved by machines, insight and understanding may be pushed beyond human abilities
